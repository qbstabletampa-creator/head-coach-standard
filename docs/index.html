  • Start with acknowledgement. If the player understands what happened, move to action.

  • Treat every newcomer as at risk until benchmarks prove otherwise.

  • Build injury prevention into the plan.

  • Get injured players safely back on the field as fast as possible.
  • Teach and coach. Do not just state the obvious.
  • Do not only say good job. Praise the fundamental that created the outcome.
  • Do not just say catch the ball. Explain why the drop happened and what fundamental fixes it.
  • Use drawings, film, walk throughs, drills, and live reps.
  • Ask players questions in meetings.
  • Use pro film when it teaches the point.
  • Laugh and keep meetings human.
  • Do not punish non hustle mistakes. Help the player fix the skill that failed.
  • Be on the details. Stress the little things.
  • When you coach a player, you are not criticizing him.
  • Be demanding in the right way.
  • Do not burn kids out. Keep them interested.
  • Do not lose through pre snap penalties, post snap penalties, or poor game management.
  • Ask before every game and decision: how can we lose this game?
  • Win fundamentals: ball security, leverage, blocking, coverage, catching, tackling.
  • Win ball traveling plays: special teams, explosives, long balls.
  • Win ball possession: turnovers, third downs, fourth downs.
  • Win points: red zone, goal line, field goals.
  • Win end of half situations: two minute, four minute, middle eight, overtime.
  • Overcome adversity during the week, travel, games, and weather delays.

  • In critical situations, think of players, both strengths and weaknesses, not plays or schemes.
  • A system should never become an extension of a coach’s ego.
  • Use your strengths against their weaknesses.
  • Get players who can win one on one matchups, then get them into those matchups.
  • Special teams should be rule based, simple, fundamentally trained, and opponent aware.
  • Defense must tackle, get off blocks, set edges, condense pockets, cover, and play the ball.
  • Offense must run the football when it wants to, get the best players the ball, and force defenses to prepare for multiple things.
  • Develop the person, not just the player.
  • Teach people skills: stand up, look people in the eye, introduce yourself, shake hands, follow up.
  • Discuss current events and teach players to know what is going on around them.
  • Teach life skills: tie a tie, use tools, change a tire, cook, write a check, build a budget.
  • Pair players with alumni or mentors connected to their career field.
  • Run networking events with alumni.
  • Teach future coaches the basics of operations, film, systems, and fundamentals.
  • Get players involved on campus and in the community.
  • Require yearly service and a yearly note writing campaign thanking someone who helped them.
  • Hire good people.
  • Hire family oriented and loyal people.
  • Hire clear communicators who respond to calls and messages.
  • Hire smart problem solvers who can use knowledge.
  • Hire great teachers who can explain knowledge in different ways to different people.
  • Hire hard workers who double check their work.
  • Hire organized people.
  • Hire good evaluators.
  • Hire character and train skill. You can teach film breakdown. You cannot teach character.
  • Look for people who love football, recognize winners, and have a plan for everything.
  • Build a team. Do not collect talent.
  • Recruiting and team building is a process. The process tells you who to offer and who to go get.
  • Build a transfer portal inside your own roster through development and retention.
  • Be honest, even when honesty costs you a recruit.
  • Evaluate film, see the body, see live, get prospects on campus, and know the person before offering.
  • Use a model for what the position should look like and compare every prospect to that model.
  • Scout with your eyes, not your ears.
  • Prioritize speed, toughness, and instincts.
  • Evaluate character, attitude, intelligence, academics, relationships, background, family, and medical history.
  • Recruit good people, smart people, hard workers, organized people, and good players.
  • Look for and players, not but players.
  • Create a calendar for the next 3 months on day one.
  • Create a calendar for the next 12 months in the first 90 days.
  • Meet with current staff members and do not rush the hiring process.
  • Identify roster needs and current commits.
  • Meet with current players and seniors.

  • Study past teams before changing the future.
  • Finalize spring, summer, schedule, facilities, equipment, and league or conference logistics.
  • Build team chemistry while assembling staff and cleaning loose ends.

Added system

Budget starter system

This fills one of the biggest head coach bible gaps without changing the original rules.

Money rhythm

A head coach needs a monthly money rhythm. Not to become an accountant. To know what the program can afford, what is coming next, and what needs fundraising before it becomes urgent.

✓ Annual plan before the year starts
✓ Monthly spend windows
✓ Track committed money, not just spent money
✓ Clear approval chain
✓ Emergency reserve

Decision rule

Every dollar should support player experience, recruiting, safety, development, or program identity. If it does not, pause it.

  • Staff
  • Recruiting
  • Travel
  • Equipment and apparel
  • Camps and meals
  • Technology
  • Facility needs
  • Fundraising events
Expansion roadmap

What still belongs in the bible

The original is strong on standards, culture, teaching, recruiting, and first steps. These are the missing operating systems a head coach eventually needs.

01

Budget And Finance

Annual budget, sport budget, recruiting budget, travel budget, camp budget, equipment budget, staff salary pool, emergency reserve, approval chain, and monthly review cadence.

02

Roster Management

Scholarship or roster allocation, depth chart health, retention risk, portal needs, walk on plan, redshirt plan, eligibility tracking, and position by position numbers.

03

Compliance And Governance

NCAA, school, league, safeguarding, recruiting windows, countable hours, discipline documentation, NIL or revenue share guardrails, and who signs off.

04

Game Week Operating System

Sunday through Saturday workflow, staff deadlines, film cutups, practice scripts, travel checklists, scouting report ownership, and game day communication.

05

Staff Management

Staff meeting rhythm, one on ones, role scorecards, evaluation cycle, staff development, conflict resolution, and succession planning.

06

Player Care And Mental Health

Mental health referral path, crisis process, family communication rules, injury support, nutrition, sleep, counseling, and chaplain or mentor support.

07

Parent, Alumni, Booster Communication

What gets communicated, how often, by whom, boundaries, donor touchpoints, alumni events, and parent issue escalation.

08

Facilities, Equipment, And Inventory

Locker room standards, equipment checkout, helmet and shoulder pad inventory, field setup, maintenance, laundry, and asset replacement cycle.

09

Data And Technology

Film system, recruiting database, GPS or sports science, academic dashboard, player notes, staff shared drive, naming conventions, and data security.

10

Brand, Media, And Public Relations

Social media rules, player media training, crisis messaging, press responsibilities, photography, graphics, and how the program tells its story.

11

Fundraising Plan

Targets, donor tiers, calendar, events, campaigns, naming opportunities, needs list, and who owns each relationship.

12

Crisis And Emergency Plan

Weather, travel, injury, player conduct, death in family, campus emergency, media issue, and decision tree for who is called first.
